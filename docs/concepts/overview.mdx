Ctrl+ArcZ protects **both sides** of a USDC payment. On the recipient side you
could send to the wrong or poisoned address; on the payer side, paying reveals
your wallet and exposes you to drainers. The system answers both with four layers
that fit together.

## The four layers

1. **Firewall (layer 1).** A pre-send scan for poisoning. This is the protection
   that actually matters against a lookalike, because the attack is that you send
   to it on purpose, so escrow cannot save you. Fail-closed: an incomplete scan is
   never reported as safe. See [Address poisoning](/concepts/poisoning) and the
   [firewall guide](/guides/firewall).
2. **Protected transfer (layer 2).** Escrow with a code-gated claim, sender
   `cancel`, and automatic refund of an expired unclaimed send, so a genuinely
   wrong send is not final. See [Protected transfer](/guides/protected-transfer).
3. **Clean history (layer 3).** Code-claimed settlement keeps your activity
   spam-free, and the contract's verified recipients feed back into layer 1 as
   known-good addresses, so a lookalike of someone you reached through a protected
   transfer is caught too.
4. **Payer-side shield (layer 4).** Disposable, policy-bound addresses funded from
   a vault. Every spend is authorized by the enclave co-signer, and the funds are
   hard-locked to the merchant and cap you chose, so the co-signer can only push
   the payment you set up or veto it. See [Payer-side shield](/concepts/payer-shield)
   and [Private Pay](/guides/private-pay).

## Where each layer kicks in

- **Before you send** to a counterparty, the firewall scans the recipient. A block
  stops the send; there is no way to lose funds to a lookalike you were about to
  copy. `sendProtected` runs this by default.
- **When you send** through the protected transfer, the funds are escrowed and the
  recipient must claim with a code you deliver out of band. If they never claim,
  the send is refundable to you.
- **After a protected transfer settles**, the recipient becomes a verified
  known-good address that strengthens the next firewall scan.
- **When you pay a merchant** and want payer-side protection, Private Pay routes
  the payment through a disposable account, and the co-signer vetoes any spend
  that fails policy or the firewall.

## Trust boundaries

The layers do not all run in the same place, and that separation is deliberate:

- **The firewall runs client-side.** It is a pure rule engine (`evaluateRisk`) fed
  by public on-chain data through `check`. It needs no secret and no trusted
  server, so anyone can reproduce its verdict. Its only dependency is a data
  source (Blockscout / ArcScan and the contract's own events), and if that source
  is down the report fails closed.
- **The co-signer runs server-side, in an enclave ("The Machine").** It holds the
  key that authorizes a spend from a disposable account. It re-reads the real
  on-chain policy rather than trusting the browser, and it vetoes by withholding
  its signature. A veto is not a warning; the spend becomes impossible. See
  [The co-signer](/guides/co-signer).
- **The contracts enforce policy on-chain.** A disposable account is hard-locked
  to one `target` and one cap, so even the co-signer can only push the exact
  payment you set up, never redirect or steal. The protected-transfer contract
  enforces the code-claim, the attempt lockout, the cancel rights, and the
  expiry refund. Nothing off-chain can override these.

The important consequence: you never sign blind, and no single component can move
your money on its own. The firewall can only refuse. The co-signer can only push
the one payment the contract already permits. The contract enforces the envelope.

## Honest positioning

This is defense-in-depth, not magic. A careful user who verifies every full
address from a trusted source does not get poisoned and does not need it. Its value
is catching the slip when discipline lapses, and making the slip non-catastrophic.
It is the category of 2FA and transaction simulators, calibrated as "a safety net
for human error and drainers," not "the unbreakable answer."

On the payer side, be precise about what is delivered. The disposable account
surface stores no payer identity, so a merchant paid from it cannot read who
funded it from the account itself. But the funding transaction (your wallet to the
disposable account) is a normal transfer on a transparent chain, so an observer can
still trace you to that account through it. Full payer privacy waits on Arc Privacy
Sector. See [Payer-side shield](/concepts/payer-shield) for the exact model.

## Why Arc

The design is only practical on a chain where **gas is USDC** (ephemeral addresses
need no separate gas), settlement is **sub-second** (the multi-step private
checkout finishes instantly), and Circle tooling (CCTP, Gateway, and the Arc
Privacy Sector roadmap) is built in.
